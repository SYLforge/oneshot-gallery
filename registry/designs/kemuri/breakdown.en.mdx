# KEMURI — breakdown

## The hook

There is an atelier in Kyoto that has been lighting the same kind of fire
since 1927, and it does not sell you the incense. It sells you the hour the
incense takes to burn. The page opens with a censer already lit — you
arrive mid-hour, the way you arrive at every real place — and the only
thing moving is a ribbon of smoke that refuses to hurry. Everything else
on the page is a way of asking you to match its pace: a poem that appears
one glyph per tick of an incense clock, paintings that take two seconds to
settle out of water, a menu that prices time instead of grams.

The design bet: on a light page where the only imagery is three generated
sumi-e plates, *simulated smoke* can carry more atmosphere than any
photograph — because a photograph of smoke is still, and stillness is the
one thing smoke never does.

## Art direction

Washi-sumi-e lives or dies on restraint. The ground is warm paper
(`#efe7d8`), the voice is ink (`#1c1814`, 14.4:1), and there is exactly
one warm color — ember `#c96f2e` — which appears where fire actually is:
the censer tip, the seals, the button's ink-fill. The moment a second warm
hue arrives, the page becomes a café menu.

Two constraints did the hard work. First, the brief's ember and gold both
fail AA on washi (2.9:1 and 2.5:1), so on light ground they were demoted
to objects — a seal is a thing, not a sentence — and two working tokens
were derived: `ink-soft #4f4a43` (sumi thinned with water, 7.1:1) for
every secondary line, and `ember-deep #a04f16` (a banked coal, 4.7:1)
wherever ember must function: eyebrows, the menu's hour numerals, the
focus ring, `::selection`. Ember gets its full voice back exactly once,
in the dark room at the end, where it measures 4.9:1 on sumi.

Second, the fonts refuse the obvious pairing. EB Garamond speaks English
the way the house would — slowly, with a good pen — and Zen Old Mincho
holds every Japanese glyph upright; the stack order (`EB Garamond, Zen
Old Mincho, serif`) means kanji fall through the Latin face into the
mincho with no markup at all. The two languages are distinguished by
posture, not size: English italicizes, mincho never leans.

## Technique 1 — curl noise, or why this smoke doesn't look like confetti

The naive way to animate smoke is to give each particle a random wander.
It reads as static, or snow — because independent random walks have no
*structure*. Real smoke is a fluid: neighboring parcels move together,
eddies form and persist, and — crucially — the flow is incompressible.
Particles never bunch into clumps or tear into voids.

You can get all of that without a fluid solver. Take any smooth scalar
field ψ (the *potential*) and define the velocity as its rotated
gradient:

```ts
// SmokeCanvas.tsx — divergence-free flow: velocity = (∂ψ/∂y, −∂ψ/∂x)
const cx =
  (psi(x, y + CURL_EPS, t) - psi(x, y - CURL_EPS, t)) / (2 * CURL_EPS);
const cy =
  -(psi(x + CURL_EPS, y, t) - psi(x - CURL_EPS, y, t)) /
  (2 * CURL_EPS);
```

This is the 2D *curl* of ψ, and a rotated gradient is divergence-free by
construction: ∂vx/∂x + ∂vy/∂y = ∂²ψ/∂x∂y − ∂²ψ/∂y∂x = 0. Whatever noise
you pour into ψ, the resulting flow conserves area — particles advected
by it swirl around the potential's contour lines forever without ever
compressing. That single property is the difference between smoke and
confetti.

ψ itself is two octaves of seeded value noise (feature sizes ~150px and
~56px), with one trick that sells the whole illusion:

```ts
// SmokeCanvas.tsx — the field itself rises with the smoke
function psi(x: number, y: number, t: number): number {
  const yr = y + t * FIELD_RISE; // 26 px/s
  return (
    valueNoise(x * EDDY_LARGE, yr * EDDY_LARGE) - 0.5 +
    (valueNoise(x * EDDY_SMALL + 37.2, yr * (EDDY_SMALL * 1.35) + 11.8) - 0.5) * 0.45
  );
}
```

Offsetting the noise domain's y by `t · 26px/s` makes the eddies
themselves drift upward. Without it, the plume swims through frozen
turbulence and looks like it's climbing a rope; with it, each coil is
born, rises, and dies with the smoke it belongs to.

Particles (a `Float32Array`, ~260–820 by stage area) don't obey the field
directly — they relax toward it at 2.4 s⁻¹, which is inertia, which is
weight. And two outside forces bend the ribbon without ever steering it:

- **Scroll.** The `useScrollVelocity` hook records raw px/ms samples; the
  rAF loop treats anything staler than 140ms as "stopped", lerps a lag
  value toward `velocity × 210px/s` (capped ±190), and applies it
  downward, scaled by height. Scroll fast and the plume bends like smoke
  behind a moved cup; stop, and it recovers on the smoothing constant,
  not on an animation you wrote.
- **Pointer.** A capped horizontal pull (≤ ±54 px/s) toward the cursor,
  gated by an energy value with asymmetric lerps — attack 0.055, release
  0.018 per 60fps frame. The smoke notices your hand in a quarter
  second and takes three to forget it. On touch, or after 2.6s of
  silence, a two-sine breeze (~30s and ~13s periods, no common divisor)
  takes back over.

The plume is drawn as three soft radial sprites: young smoke uses a
darker ink sprite that crossfades to pale gray as it disperses, and an
ember glint rides the first half-second of each particle's life — the
coal's light climbing into the smoke. Before first paint the system
pre-advects 120 steps of 85ms, so you never see the plume being born; it
was burning before you arrived.

## Technique 2 — the ink that settles

The three plates are generated sumi-e paintings (ComfyUI; see PROMPT.md's
image-recipe), but they are not dropped in as flat `<img>` tags — that
would have erased the signature interaction. Instead each painting is
embedded as an SVG `<image>` inside the same per-plate art group that the
hand-drawn vector version used, so the displacement-settle filter still
owns it. (The plates first shipped as layered vector ink — silhouettes
under `feGaussianBlur` at several radii for wet edges, opacity as aerial
perspective, the smoke stroke in plate three drawn three times like a
loaded brush retracing itself. The art was swapped to raster for richer
sumi texture; the filter wrapper is unchanged.) On top of each art group
sits the reveal:

```tsx
{/* InkPlates.tsx — per-plate turbulence feeding a displacement map.
    The <image> inside the filtered <g> is the generated painting;
    the feDisplacementMap acts on it exactly as it did on vector ink. */}
<filter id="kemuri-p1-settle" x="-14%" y="-14%" width="128%" height="128%">
  <feTurbulence type="fractalNoise" baseFrequency="0.011 0.017" numOctaves="2" seed="19" result="w" />
  <feDisplacementMap in="SourceGraphic" in2="w" scale="0" xChannelSelector="R" yChannelSelector="G" />
</filter>
```

The markup ships with `scale="0"` — settled. That is the page's no-JS
state and its reduced-motion state: a finished painting. Only after mount
does JavaScript raise the chaos, and only when a plate enters the
viewport does it settle:

```ts
// InkPlates.tsx — animate ONE number: the displacement scale
const tick = (now: number) => {
  const p = Math.min((now - t0) / SETTLE_MS, 1);
  const ease = 1 - (1 - p) * (1 - p) * (1 - p); // cubic out — a sigh
  fe.setAttribute("scale", String(SETTLE_FROM * (1 - ease)));
  if (p < 1) rafs.add(requestAnimationFrame(tick));
};
```

The perf shape matters: `baseFrequency` and `seed` never change, so the
browser rasterizes the turbulence once and caches it; each frame of the
settle pays only the displacement pass. Animating `baseFrequency` instead
(the tempting knob) recomputes the noise every frame and stutters on
mid-range hardware. At scale 110 the painting is weather; at 0 it is ink;
the cubic ease means it crosses "recognizable" early and spends most of
its 1900ms on the last few pixels — which is exactly how wet ink dries.

## Technique 3 — the incense clock's cadence

Split text is an accessibility trap with a well-known shape: glyph spans
read as letter salad in a screen reader. Every split string on this page
— the wordmark, the vertical poem — keeps its original in the accessible
tree and animates only copies:

```tsx
{/* Cadence.tsx — the real string for AT, aria-hidden glyphs for the eye */}
<span className="kemuri-sr">{text}</span>
<span aria-hidden="true" className="kemuri-cadence__row">
  {glyphs.map((g, i) => (
    <span className="kemuri-cadence__g"
      style={{ "--kd": `${start + i * step}ms` } as CSSProperties}>
      {g}
    </span>
  ))}
</span>
```

Each glyph carries its delay as a custom property, and the *only* thing
JavaScript does at reveal time is add one class to one container — the
IntersectionObserver never touches a glyph. From there CSS runs the whole
choreography: `transition-delay: var(--kd)` staggers the glyphs (60ms per
glyph, 620ms per column for the poem — the pacing of an incense clock,
deliberately slower than any UI convention), and the pre-reveal state
(`opacity: 0`, a 5px blur, a 0.16em drop) exists only under the
`.kemuri-js` root class. No JavaScript, no hidden poetry.

The poem's Japanese is set in `writing-mode: vertical-rl`, which quietly
does the right thing: DOM order is reading order, columns flow right to
left, and `line-height: 2.5` becomes column pitch — the 間 between lines
is typeset, not positioned.

## Motion math

Two numbers give the smoke its temperament:

- **Velocity relaxation 2.4 s⁻¹.** Per frame, a particle covers
  `1 − e^(−dt·2.4)` of the gap between its velocity and the field's —
  a half-life of ~290ms. That lag is the difference between smoke
  (carried) and sparks (obedient). Framerate-independent by
  construction, because it's computed from `dt`, not per-frame.
- **Attack 0.055 / release 0.018.** The pointer-energy lerps, normalized
  to 60fps. Half-life ≈ 12 frames in, ≈ 38 frames out. Attention arrives
  in a breath and leaves like the smell of smoke — which is the brand,
  expressed in two constants.

Everything else obeys two easings: `ease-smoke`
(`cubic-bezier(0.22,1,0.36,1)`) front-loads its travel and spends the
tail settling sub-pixel, so 900–1150ms reads as calm rather than slow;
`ease-ink` (`cubic-bezier(0.65,0,0.35,1)`) is symmetric and weighted for
the two "physical" moves — the loader's paper lift and the button's ink
fill. Ambient cycles (ember breath 5.6s, hint drift 7.4s) are mutually
prime with each other and with both cadences, so the idle page never
visibly loops.

## Performance notes

- One rAF loop for the smoke, throttled to ~64fps (`FRAME_MS = 15.5`),
  `dt` clamped to 48ms so a background tab never causes a lurch on
  return. The loop stops via IntersectionObserver (80px margin) *and*
  `visibilitychange` — zero frames offscreen or tab-hidden.
- DPR capped at 2; particle count scales with stage area (260–820).
  A particle costs 8 value-noise evaluations for the curl (4 ψ samples ×
  2 octaves) and at most three `drawImage` calls of pre-rendered
  gradient sprites — no per-particle `createRadialGradient`, no shadows.
- The warmup (120 × 85ms steps) runs once, synchronously, at mount:
  ~10s of simulated time for about a frame's worth of work.
- The plates animate a single SVG attribute; turbulence is computed once
  per plate. The washi grain is one static `feTurbulence` rect — filters
  never animate anywhere on the page.
- All CSS animation is `transform`, `opacity`, `filter`. Reveals write
  `class` + `transitionDelay` from one observer callback; no React
  re-renders on scroll. The page re-renders exactly twice after mount:
  loader lift start, loader unmount.

## Accessibility notes

- **Reduced motion:** the loader never mounts (and `display: none` as a
  belt), the smoke warms up and paints one composed still (the plume
  mid-air, ember lit), the plates stay at `scale=0` — never disturbed —
  and a media query flattens every transition and ambient cycle. The
  result is a finished painting, not a paused video.
- **No-JS:** every pre-reveal style is gated behind `.kemuri-js`, added
  on mount; the displacement chaos is only *raised* by JavaScript. The
  SSR page is the completed page.
- **Semantics:** the canvas stage is `role="img"` with a bilingual EN/JA
  description; each plate SVG is `role="img"` with its own; split text
  keeps visually-hidden originals; the wordmark is an `h1` with
  `aria-label`; decorative seals and the censer are `aria-hidden`.
- **Keyboard & touch:** the two interactive elements (button, top link)
  take a 2px ember ring on `:focus-visible` — `ember-deep` on washi,
  `ember` in the dark room, both AA for non-text. The smoke self-drifts
  on touch; nothing anywhere lives behind hover.
- **Contrast:** sumi 14.4:1, ink-soft 7.1:1, ember-deep 4.7:1 on washi;
  washi 14.4:1, washi-dim 7.9:1, ember 4.9:1 on sumi. Smoke gray and
  gold never carry meaning. Worst case under a passing wisp, the
  wordmark still reads ≈ 8:1.

## Remix ideas

1. **Tea house.** Keep the curl field; make the ribbon *steam* — shorter
   life, tighter eddies (halve both feature sizes), rising from a cup at
   the ledger's edge. Palette swaps ember for a deep matcha; the poem
   becomes the order of a tea ceremony.
2. **Ink stone.** Invert to a sumi ground and run the smoke *downward* —
   ink diffusing into water (negate buoyancy, triple relaxation). The
   plates then assemble instead of settling: displacement scale runs
   0 → 110 → 0 as each enters, a drop hitting the stone and clearing.
3. **Bonshō at midnight.** Replace the plume with a struck temple bell:
   the curl field's gain pulses once per strike (SWIRL × a decaying
   sine), the glyph cadence syncs to the strike interval, and the menu
   lists the 108 tolls of New Year's Eve — sold, of course, one toll at
   a time.
