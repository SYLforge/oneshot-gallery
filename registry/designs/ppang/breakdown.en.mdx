# PPANG! — breakdown

## The hook

There is a bakery in Eunpyeong-gu that opens at 4 AM, not because it has
to, but because the owner's grandmother did, and the hour the bread wakes
up turned out to be hereditary. The page does not sell you bread. It is
*one chapter of a webtoon* about that hour. You scroll the way you scroll
a Naver Webtoon: an establishing shot of the bakery under a still-dark sky,
the baker introduced by a speech bubble, the signature loaf named, the
kitchen narrated over a wide shot, and a colophon that tells you where to
find the place at dawn. The dawn itself is a character — the sky behind
the hero climbs from indigo to gold as you read, and by the time you reach
the kitchen the morning has arrived.

The design bet: a page can be **a story about a place** without being a
shop, and generated illustration earns its place when every panel is a
drawing in a chapter — revealed panel-by-panel, captioned like a webtoon,
never a grid of stock-looking cards.

## Art direction

Webtoon lives on warmth and rhythm. The ground is cream paper
(`#faf6ee`), the voice is warm brown ink (`#3d2817`, 12.0:1), and there is
exactly one warm accent — apricot `#e89b4c` — which appears where dawn
light is: the OPEN lamp on the sign, the SFX stamp, the focus ring. The
moment a second warm hue carries meaning, the page becomes a bakery menu.

Two constraints did the hard work. First, the accent apricot measures only
3.0:1 on cream, so it is **never functional text on paper** — everywhere
the accent must speak it is banked to `amber-deep #a85f1c` (4.8:1, AA):
section eyebrows, the hours' time numerals, the focus ring, the
`::selection` ground. Apricot stays a surface and a large-display color.
Second, the hero text sits over a dark scrim (a webtoon lower-third) so
light text is AA at *every* dawn phase — robust against whichever of the
three sky gradients is showing, and authentic to how webtoons caption
their panels.

The type refuses the obvious pairing. Black Han Sans is the Korean
display voice — the wordmark 빵!, panel titles, SFX lettering — loud and
warm, the cover of a chapter. Noto Serif KR holds every Korean body glyph
in a warm serif, so the narration reads like a letter, not a menu.
Fraunces is the English voice, italic, never larger than 0.62em of its
Korean companion — the two languages distinguished by posture, the
English leaning, the Korean standing. Stacks always lead with the Korean
face, so Hangul never falls through to a default sans.

## Technique 1 — the panel that wipes open (clip-path-reveal)

The naive way to reveal an image is a fade. It reads as a slideshow. A
webtoon panel doesn't fade in — it *arrives*, like a new cut being laid on
the page. So every illustration reveals with a `clip-path` wipe, and each
of the three panels uses a different direction so the cuts never repeat:

```css
/* styles.css — three directions, one per panel */
.ppang-js [data-reveal="panel"]:not(.is-visible) .ppang-panel__mount {
  clip-path: inset(0 100% 0 0);       /* baker: left → right */
}
.ppang-product [data-reveal="panel"]:not(.is-visible) .ppang-panel__mount {
  clip-path: inset(48% 48% 48% 48%);  /* signature: center → out */
}
.ppang-kitchen [data-reveal="panel"]:not(.is-visible) .ppang-panel__mount {
  clip-path: inset(0 0 100% 0);       /* kitchen: top → bottom */
}
```

The wipe lives on the panel's paper *mount*, not the art — the image
itself is never displaced, only unclipped, so there's zero layout shift
and no re-rasterization of the illustration. The transition is 1100ms on
`ease-page` (`cubic-bezier(0.16,1,0.3,1)`), an easing that front-loads
its travel and spends the tail settling sub-pixel, so the wipe reads as
*deliberate* rather than slow. `clip-path` is compositor-friendly (it
never touches layout), and the `is-visible` class is handed over by a
single `IntersectionObserver` in `useReveal` — one observer for the whole
page, no per-panel JS.

The markup ships with the panels un-clipped (the `:not(.is-visible)` rule
only applies under `.ppang-js`), so with JavaScript off every illustration
is simply there. Under reduced motion the media query forces `clip-path:
none` on every panel mount — the chapter is a printed comic, not a paused
animation.

## Technique 2 — the narrator reading aloud (char-split-reveal)

A caption that appears all at once reads as a label. A caption that
arrives glyph by glyph reads as *someone speaking*. Every narration line
on the page — the baker's speech bubble, the kitchen caption, the
product name — is split per Hangul syllable / Latin letter, with the real
string kept whole for assistive tech:

```tsx
/* Narration.tsx — the real string for AT, aria-hidden glyphs for the eye */
<span className="ppang-sr">{text}</span>
<span aria-hidden="true" className="ppang-narration__row">
  {glyphs.map((g, i) => (
    <span className="ppang-narration__g"
      style={{ "--pn": `${start + i * step}ms` }} as CSSProperties}>
      {g === " " ? "\u00A0" : g}
    </span>
  ))}
</span>
```

Each glyph carries its delay as a custom property (`--pn`), and the
*only* thing JavaScript does at reveal time is add one class to the
panel — the observer never touches a glyph. From there CSS runs the
choreography: `transition-delay: var(--pn)` staggers the glyphs (38ms
each for narration, 90ms for the product name), and the pre-reveal state
(`opacity: 0`, a 0.5em drop) exists only under the `.ppang-js` root
*and* only while the nearest panel is not yet `is-visible`. So no
narration speaks before its panel has wiped open — the caption follows
the drawing, the way a webtoon works.

`Array.from` does the split, so Hangul syllables and jamo clusters
survive as single code points (a syllable never breaks across spans);
spaces become no-break spaces so a glyph never collapses at a line edge.
The English line arrives half a beat after the Korean, by its own
`--pn` delay — the second voice, leaning in.

## Technique 3 — the diorama (pointer-parallax + scroll depth)

The hero is not a flat frame; it's a diorama. Four layers drift at
different rates as you scroll (the webtoon's depth) and, on fine
pointers, lean toward the cursor:

```css
/* styles.css — each layer gets its own depth multiplier */
.ppang-hero__far   { transform: translate3d(calc(var(--ppang-px) * -6px),
                       calc(var(--ppang-py) * -4px + var(--ppang-sky-y) * 0.4), 0); }
.ppang-hero__art   { transform: translate3d(calc(var(--ppang-px) * 10px),
                       calc(var(--ppang-py) * 6px + var(--ppang-sky-y) * 0.7), 0); }
.ppang-hero__sign  { transform: translate3d(calc(var(--ppang-px) * 18px),
                       calc(var(--ppang-py) * 12px + var(--ppang-sky-y) * 1.1), 0); }
```

The far hill drifts slowest, the hanging sign fastest — the depth that
sells the illusion. The cap lives in CSS (±6 / ±10 / ±18 px on pointer,
60px on scroll), next to the layers. Two hooks feed the variables:
`usePointerParallax` writes `--ppang-px/py ∈ [-1,1]` from the cursor
(lerp 0.08/frame, parks itself when settled and the pointer has left);
`useScrollProgress` writes `--ppang-sky-y` from how far the hero has
left the viewport. Both are transform-only — the only per-frame writes
on the whole page are four CSS custom properties on the root, all
consumed by `transform`. On touch, the pointer hook disables itself
(the diorama stays still); on coarse pointers you still get the scroll
parallax. Under reduced motion, all four variables are pinned to 0 and
the diorama is a composed still.

The sign is an inline SVG (real Hangul 빵! in the loaded display face, a
small OPEN lamp that breathes) painted over the awning — the
illustration carries no text by design, so the actual signage is crisp
vector at any resolution, and fully accessible (`role="img"` with a
bilingual label). It sways ±1.1° on a 6.2s cycle; the lamp breathes on a
5.3s cycle; the two share no common divisor, so the idle hero never
visibly loops.

## The dawn, scrubbed by scroll

The signature backdrop: as you scroll the first 55% of the page, the sky
behind the hero climbs from pre-dawn indigo through dawn blush to morning
gold. Rather than animate gradient stops (expensive, jittery), the sky is
**three stacked gradient elements whose opacity is the crossfade**:

```css
/* styles.css — phase A (indigo) fades out as dawn passes 0.5 */
.ppang-hero__sky::before {
  background: linear-gradient(180deg, #2a2547, var(--ppang-dawn-indigo), …);
  opacity: calc(1 - clamp(var(--ppang-dawn) * 2, 0, 1));
}
/* phase C (gold) fades in as dawn passes 0.5 */
.ppang-hero__gold {
  background: linear-gradient(180deg, #c99a6a, #e9bf7e, …);
  opacity: clamp((var(--ppang-dawn) - 0.5) * 2, 0, 1);
}
```

`--ppang-dawn` is a single number in `[0,1]`, written once per rAF by
`useScrollProgress` from `scrollY / maxScroll` (clamped to the first 55%
of the page — by the kitchen, the morning has arrived and holds). The
blush phase peaks in the middle of the dawn, computed as
`1 - max(dawn - 0.5, 0.5 - dawn) * 2` — a triangular peak built from
`max()`/`min()`, deliberately avoiding `abs()` (newer and unevenly
supported) for the same result. With JS off, `--ppang-dawn` stays at its
declared default of 0 — the hero opens on full pre-dawn indigo, which is
the chapter's correct opening beat. Under reduced motion it's pinned to
1 — morning has arrived.

## The generated illustrations

The four panels are generated in ComfyUI: the Illustrious-XL merge
`zenijiMixKWebtoon_v10` with the `toonystarkKoreanWebtoonFlux` LoRA at
0.75, 832×1216 / 1024×1024 / 1024×768, 30 steps, CFG 6.5, Euler a, each
upscaled 2× with RealESRGAN. The full recipe ships in `image-recipe.md`
and the exported graph in `workflows/`.

Two decisions matter. First, **no image carries text** — by prompt and by
negative prompt — so the bakery's name, the OPEN lamp, the SFX (쫀득!),
and every caption are real HTML/SVG/CSS. Hangul stays crisp vector at any
resolution, screen readers get real text, and the images can be
regenerated (or swapped to an audited checkpoint) without retyping a
single label. Second, the license audit: the checkpoint is **not yet
audited** in `docs/model-licenses.md`, which is a G4 gate blocker until a
maintainer co-signs an audit row. The entry is staged for that audit;
swapping checkpoints later regenerates only the four files in
`/media/ppang/`.

## Motion math

- **Glyph cadence 38ms.** The narrator's reading pace — slower than a
  typewriter (which is ~30ms), fast enough to feel like speech. The
  product name uses 90ms because it is a *title*, not narration.
- **Pointer lerp 0.08/frame** (half-life ≈ 8 frames at 60fps). The
  diorama notices the cursor in a blink and takes a moment to settle —
  drift, not obedience. Computed from `dt` so it's framerate-independent.
- **Panel wipe 1100ms, caption 800ms at +350ms.** The caption begins
  only after the panel is meaningfully open, so the eye reads the
  drawing first and the words second — exactly the order a webtoon
  imposes.
- The two ambient cycles (sway 6.2s, lamp 5.3s) and the hint drift (4.6s)
  are mutually prime, so the idle hero never visibly loops.

## Performance notes

- One `IntersectionObserver` for every reveal on the page (`useReveal`),
  threshold 0.16 — no per-element observers, no React re-renders on
  scroll. The observer only writes `class` + `transitionDelay`.
- `useScrollProgress` runs a single rAF, throttled to fire only on
  scroll/resize/visibility, and writes two custom properties; it pauses
  entirely on `visibilitychange` (hidden tab). `dt` is clamped to 48ms.
- `usePointerParallax` runs its rAF *only while settling* — it parks
  itself when the lerp converges and the pointer has left, so an idle
  hero costs zero frames.
- Every animation is `transform`, `opacity`, `filter`, or `clip-path`.
  The four per-frame CSS-variable writes are all consumed by `transform`.
  Zero layout-property animation anywhere.
- Images: AVIF `<source>` + WebP `<img>` fallback, dimensions reserved
  (no CLS), `loading="lazy"` + `decoding="async"` except the hero
  (`eager`/`sync`, the first paint). Total payload ≈ 582 KB AVIF — well
  under the 5120 KB budget.

## Accessibility notes

- **Reduced motion:** `--ppang-dawn` pinned at 1 (morning arrived), all
  parallax variables at 0, all ambient cycles killed, every pre-reveal
  state forced to final composition (panels `clip-path: none`, captions
  `opacity: 1`, glyphs visible). The page reads as a printed chapter.
- **No-JS:** every pre-reveal style is gated behind `.ppang-js`, added on
  mount. The SSR page is the completed chapter — every illustration and
  every line visible.
- **Semantics:** the hero wordmark is an `h1` with `aria-label`; split
  text keeps visually-hidden originals; the SVG sign is `role="img"`
  with a bilingual label; decorative elements (far hill, lamp, hint,
  SFX) are `aria-hidden`.
- **Keyboard & touch:** the two interactive elements (address link,
  top link) take a 2px amber-deep ring on `:focus-visible`. The parallax
  self-disables on coarse pointers; the dawn still scrubs on scroll.
- **Contrast:** ink 12.0:1, ink-soft 6.6:1, amber-deep 4.8:1 on paper;
  hero text over the dark scrim clears AA at every dawn phase. Apricot
  is never functional text on cream.

## Remix ideas

1. **Late-night ramen counter.** Keep the panel-wipe grammar and the
   scroll-scrubbed sky (sunset → night → steam), swap the bakery for a
   six-seat counter. The speech bubble becomes the chef's two-line
   greeting; the SFX becomes the slap of noodles.
2. **Bookshop closing up.** Invert the dawn: the sky scrubs *down* from
   dusk to night as you scroll, the panels wipe closed instead of open
   (`inset(0)` → `inset(0 100% 0 0)`), and the colophon lists the books
   that found homes that day.
3. **Webtoon itself.** Promote the panels into a true vertical-cut
   sequence (8–12 panels), let the scroll *scrub* the wipe progress
   directly (`clip-path inset` tied to scroll progress, not a one-shot
   observer), and add an SFX-on-stomp interaction per panel.
