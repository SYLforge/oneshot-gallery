# CHADŌ 茶道 — breakdown

## The hook

A tea ceremony is not a product; it is a practiced pause. CHADŌ commits to
that premise at the extreme: the most restrained, most empty, most slow
entry in the gallery, where the only spectacle is the discipline of what
is left out. The page is a virtual chanoyu — the four-hour rite compressed
into one scroll, paced in breaths. The hero's title 茶道 climbs a vertical
column in tategaki, the authentic Japanese reading direction that almost
no Western "Japanese-inspired" site uses. A matcha orb breathes on a 4-4-4
cycle. A bamboo whisk draws itself as you scroll, until one bowl of tea
blooms into being. Then the bowl is set down, and it ends.

The design bet: on a gallery full of motion pieces, the slowest entry —
where motion is almost forbidden and the craft is the *ma* between beats —
can hold its own by being unmistakably itself. The restraint is the craft.

## Art direction

Everything happens against aged washi (`#e8e2d4`), with a warmer lit skin
(`#ece7da`) for the hero and climax and a darker recessed paper
(`#d9d2c2`) for the tokonoma alcove. Warm sumi ink (`#2b2620`, 11.61:1)
does the speaking — never pure black, because wabi-sabi avoids the harsh.
The single accent is matcha green `#5d6b3a`, the one living color on the
page. It appears only as small, expensive things: the whisk's bamboo
tines, the bowl's tea surface, a single ikebana bloom, a hairline of
emphasis, the focus ring.

There is one contrast decision that shaped everything: matcha `#5d6b3a`
on washi measures 4.48:1 — just under the AA body threshold of 4.5. The
brief asked explicitly to verify it. So matcha is restricted to decorative
and large uses (strokes, fills, hairlines), and a derived `matcha-deep
#4a5630` (6.10:1) carries any word that would otherwise fall short — the
beat numerals, the bowl's romanized name, the closing seal. The brand
accent stays `#5d6b3a`; the text-grade token keeps the page honest.

Two serifs, two roles. Shippori Mincho carries every CJK glyph — chosen
because its vertical metrics were drawn first, so it sets the tategaki
title and the alcove scroll poem without complaint. Cormorant Garamond
answers in Latin: the wordmark, the English annotations, the colophon, the
romanized breath beats. The family stack does quiet work — Cormorant first,
Shippori second — so kanji fall through into the Japanese serif with no
markup.

## The signature — tategaki (縦書き)

The hero title 茶道 is set in `writing-mode: vertical-rl; text-orientation:
upright` — vertical text, the authentic Japanese reading direction. This is
the most under-used authentic Japanese interaction on the web; the
gallery's asian-aesthetics reference names it a Tier-1 differentiator that
"almost no Western Japanese-inspired site uses" (§4). The two kanji split
into `aria-hidden` spans behind an `aria-label`, each carrying its index as
a custom property, so the header can write itself top-to-bottom — the only
direction a brush ever travels:

```tsx
<h1 className="chado-tategaki" lang="ja" aria-label="茶道（さどう）">
  {TITLE.map((ch, i) => (
    <span aria-hidden="true" className="chado-tategaki__ch"
      style={{ "--ch-ci": i } as CSSProperties}>{ch}</span>
  ))}
</h1>
```

```css
.chado-tategaki {
  writing-mode: vertical-rl;
  text-orientation: upright;
  font-size: clamp(7rem, 18vw, 14rem);
}
.chado-js .chado-tategaki__ch {
  animation: ch-tategaki-rise 1300ms var(--ch-ease-tea) both;
  animation-delay: calc(180ms + var(--ch-ci, 0) * 220ms);
}
```

Tategaki is kept deliberately scarce — it appears exactly twice (the hero
title and the tokonoma scroll) so it reads as sacred rather than decorative.
Under reduced motion the glyphs render statically (no rise); the vertical
layout is unchanged, because the writing mode is the content, not the
animation.

## Technique — the breath-paced cue (scroll-scrub-pinned's sibling)

The page is paced by a hero breathing cue on a 4-4-4 box-breath cycle:
inhale 4s · hold 4s · exhale 4s · rest 2s — a 14-second bow, the pace of
someone breathing opposite the kettle. A matcha orb scales with the breath;
under it a romanized label (`吸う · 止む · 吐く · 間`) cross-fades as the phase
turns. The whole page is implicitly paced by it: content arrives, then
waits. The opposite of busy.

The cycle is driven by a phase index, so the scale (CSS) and the label
(React) never drift apart:

```ts
// useBreathPace — one setTimeout per phase, not per frame
const tick = () => {
  phaseIdx = ((phaseIdx + 1) % 4) as BreathPhase;
  setPhase(phaseIdx);
  timeoutId = setTimeout(tick, BREATH_DURATIONS[phaseIdx]);
};
```

The scale itself is pure CSS keyed off `data-breath={phase}`, so it runs on
the compositor (transform only). The cycle pauses when the hero leaves the
viewport (an IntersectionObserver in `page.tsx` flips `cueActive`) and when
the tab is hidden, so no timer runs for an empty room. Under reduced motion
the hook pins the phase to "rest" and the orb simply sits, full and still.

This is the honest reading of "breath-paced scroll." Forcibly throttling
native scroll velocity (wheel hijacking) is an accessibility hazard and
would fight `prefers-reduced-motion`; instead the cue achieves the
experiential goal — guiding the user to slow down — through pacing and vast
*ma*, while leaving scroll control entirely with the user.

## Technique — the chasen draws itself (scroll-scrub-pinned + svg-line-draw)

The matcha preparation is a brush-stroke draw-on. The chashitsu (tea
preparation scene) pins with `position: sticky` while the four-step temae
text flows past, and a scroll-scrubbed chasen draws its bamboo tines into a
bowl. Every tine is a `pathLength=1` path whose dashoffset is the inverse of
scroll progress:

```css
.chado-chasen__tine {
  stroke-dasharray: 1 1;
  stroke-dashoffset: calc(1 - var(--ch-scrub, 1));
}
/* the tea surface blooms only as the preparation completes:
   scrub 0.8 → 1 maps to opacity 0 → 1, browser clamps for free */
.chado-bowl__tea {
  fill: var(--ch-matcha);
  opacity: calc(var(--ch-scrub, 1) * 5 - 4);
}
```

The pin is native sticky, so no layout is scripted:

```css
@media (min-width: 900px) {
  .chado-chashitsu__plate {
    position: sticky;
    top: clamp(4rem, 12vh, 7rem);
    align-self: start;
  }
}
```

The scrub is a small rAF loop. Each frame does exactly one read (the
scene's rect) and one write (a custom property) — read before write, so
layout is never thrashed — and the raw progress is lerp-smoothed with a
dt-normalized factor so a 120Hz display does not scrub twice as fast as a
60Hz one:

```ts
// useScrollProgress — one read, one lerp, one write
const center = rect.top + rect.height / 2;
const span = Math.max(Math.min(rect.height * 0.9, vh * 1.2), 1);
const raw = (vh * 0.62 - center) / span;
const target = Math.min(1, Math.max(0, raw));
value += (target - value) * (1 - Math.pow(0.88, dt / 16.7));
el.style.setProperty("--ch-scrub", value.toFixed(4));
```

An IntersectionObserver starts and stops the loop, and a `visibilitychange`
listener stops it when the tab is hidden, so an offscreen chashitsu costs
zero frames. The `vh * 1.2` cap matters on mobile: single-column chashitsu
get tall, and without the cap the whisk would still be drawing long after
the bowl scrolled away.

Under `prefers-reduced-motion` the hook writes `--ch-scrub: 1` once and a
media query sets the plate to `position: static` — a completed preparation,
statically waiting. Without JavaScript the CSS default does the same: every
tine is drawn and the bowl is full.

The tokonoma's ikebana is the page's one *time-based* draw (every other
stroke is scroll-scrubbed or static): a single stem and leaf that draw once
on reveal, staggered, so the alcove arrives assembled.

## Technique — aged washi paper (feturbulence-texture)

The paper is a single full-viewport SVG `feTurbulence` pass —
`fractalNoise`, baseFrequency 0.62, 2 octaves, seed 17 — mapped by
`feColorMatrix` to sumi at ≤4.5% alpha and multiplied over everything. It
is aged fibers, not film grain, and it never animates:

```jsx
<filter id="chado-grain-f">
  <feTurbulence type="fractalNoise" baseFrequency="0.62"
    numOctaves={2} seed="17" stitchTiles="stitch" />
  <feColorMatrix type="matrix"
    values="0 0 0 0 0.17  0 0 0 0 0.15  0 0 0 0 0.125  0 0 0 0.045 0" />
</filter>
```

A warm age-vignette (`radial-gradient` to `rgba(91,80,58,0.06)` at the
edges) makes the page read as paper yellowed evenly in a drawer. The
tokonoma adds its own paper: a darker `tokonoma` ground, a 1px sumi frame
rebated 2.2vw in, and a second `feTurbulence` (baseFrequency 0.04) only
inside the recess — so the alcove reads as deeper, older paper. Stillness
is the material; no grain moves.

## The ma between beats

Where a landing page stacks features, CHADŌ stacks pauses. The four
ceremony beats — roji, tsukubai, wa-kei-sei-jaku, the host's bow — sit
with `clamp(6rem, 18vh, 12rem)` of air between them, the largest inter-
element gap in the gallery. Section padding runs 22–26vh. Each beat is a
short bilingual statement with vast negative space around it; the section
exists so that the white between statements can exist. This is the *ma*
(間) principle made layout: the meaningful pause is the design.

## Accessibility notes

- **Reduced motion:** the breath orb sits full and still (no rings, no
  label animation), the tategaki renders statically (no glyph rise, the
  vertical layout intact), every chasen tine is drawn and the plate
  unpins (`position: static`), the ikebana stem is drawn without its
  transition, the bowl's steam is still, and reveals are present without
  motion. One calm, fully-composed static ceremony.
- **No-JS:** every pre-reveal state is gated behind `.chado-js`, added on
  mount. With JavaScript off the page is a finished document — a tea rite
  degrades to paper. The `--ch-scrub` default of 1 leaves the whisk drawn
  and the bowl full.
- **Semantics:** the tategaki `h1` keeps `茶道（さどう）` in `aria-label` and
  hides its animated spans; SVG scenes are `role="img"` with bilingual
  `<title>` / `<figcaption>`; the beats are an ordered list; focus is a
  2px `matcha-deep` `:focus-visible` ring.
- **Contrast:** sumi 11.61:1, ink-soft 4.58:1, matcha-deep 6.10:1 — all AA
  or better. The 4.48:1 matcha and the 1.4:1 rule never carry a word.

## Performance notes

- Two timers exist on the page: one `setTimeout` chain for the breath cue
  (one tick per phase, not per frame) and one rAF loop for the chasen
  scrub, alive only while its chashitsu intersects a generous observer
  margin and the tab is visible. Each scrub frame is one
  `getBoundingClientRect` and one custom-property write.
- Everything animated is `transform`, `opacity`, or `stroke-dashoffset`;
  the pin is native sticky, not scripted layout.
- Reveals write `class` + `transitionDelay` imperatively from one
  IntersectionObserver — no React re-renders on scroll anywhere.
- Zero images, zero dependencies; the heaviest assets are two font
  families, and Latin/CJK subsetting is left to next/font.

## Remix ideas

1. **The incense clock.** Replace tea with a graded incense trail that
   burns down as you scroll; the breath cue becomes the
   Koh-doh (incense-way) guest's measured inhale. Tategaki keeps the
   headers; the bowl becomes a censer.
2. **Seasonal kisetsu.** TAWARAYA's "twelve months" move: the palette
   drifts through the year (cherry, verdant, maple, snow) while the
   ceremony structure stays fixed. One accent color per season; the
   bowl changes.
3. **The tea lesson.** Turn the four beats into four clickable lessons
   where each pin reveals the next step of temae on demand (not on
   scroll) — a calm, self-paced tea school. The breath cue paces each
   lesson's reading time.
